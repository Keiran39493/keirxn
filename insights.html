What it means

VO₂ Max is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during maximal effort. It is the single strongest predictor of cardiovascular endurance and a key longevity biomarker. Elite marathon runners typically score 70–85 mL/kg/min. You can estimate yours with the Cooper 12-minute run: VO₂ Max ≈ (distance in m − 504.9) ÷ 44.73.

How to improve
  • Zone 2 cardio 3–5× per week (30–60 min) — the highest ROI training modality
  • Add one weekly VO₂ Max interval session (4–6 × 4 min at 90–95% max HR)
  • Gradually increase weekly training volume (no more than 10% per week)
  • Lose excess body fat — VO₂ Max is expressed per kg of bodyweight
  • Prioritise sleep — VO₂ Max adaptations occur overnight

HRV — Heart Rate Variability
ms
Enter your age to see age-adjusted percentiles.
—
What it means

HRV measures millisecond-level variation between heartbeats. A high HRV reflects a well-functioning autonomic nervous system, capable of switching between sympathetic (stress) and parasympathetic (recovery) states. It is used by elite sports teams as the primary daily readiness metric — a single score matters less than the trend relative to your personal baseline.

How to improve
  • Zone 2 cardio 3–4× per week — the most direct HRV driver
  • Consistent sleep and wake times, including weekends
  • Breathwork: 5.5 s inhale / 5.5 s exhale (resonance breathing)
  • Cold exposure — brief cold showers activate vagal tone
  • Alcohol significantly suppresses HRV for 24–48 hours
  • Reduce HRV-stressing workouts when the score is >15% below baseline

What it means

Resting HR is measured at complete rest, ideally first thing in the morning. It reflects how efficiently your heart pumps blood — a stronger heart ejects more blood per beat, requiring fewer beats per minute. Professional endurance athletes routinely sit at 40–50 bpm. Your RHR also activates personalised Karvonen training zones in the card above.

How to improve
  • Sustained aerobic training — even 30 min of brisk walking daily reduces RHR
  • Zone 2 volume is most effective for cardiac adaptation
  • Reduce body weight — every kg of fat demands extra cardiac output
  • Limit caffeine, especially in the afternoon
  • Manage chronic stress — cortisol chronically elevates heart rate
  • Track morning RHR — sudden rises of >7 bpm signal illness or overtraining
Recovery & Readiness
Body Battery — Recovery Score
/100
—
0 25 50 75 100
What it means

The recovery score is a composite 0–100 metric calculated from overnight HRV, sleep duration and quality, resting heart rate, and accumulated stress. It fills during sleep and drains through physical and psychological demands throughout the day. Treat it as a daily readiness signal — not just a number, but an input for deciding training intensity.

Reading the score
  • 75–100: Fully recovered — push hard
  • 50–74: Good reserves — moderate to high intensity OK
  • 25–49: Low — light activity only
  • 0–24: Critical — rest is essential today
How to improve
  • Prioritise 7–9 hours of uninterrupted sleep — the primary recharge mechanism
  • Avoid intense training when score is below 25
  • 10–20 min naps can partially restore the score mid-day
  • Manage daily stress — psychological load drains the score as much as physical work
  • Avoid alcohol within 3 hours of sleep — it collapses recovery quality

What it means

Sleep score aggregates overall sleep quality 0–100 from duration, sleep stage composition (deep, REM, light, awake), overnight HRV, and resting HR. Deep sleep drives physical repair and growth hormone release; REM consolidates skill and memory. A single night below 6 hours measurably reduces reaction time, strength output, and cognitive performance the following day.

How to improve

  • No screens 60 min before bed, or use blue-light blocking glasses
  • No caffeine after 14:00 — half-life is 5–7 hours
  • Avoid alcohol within 3 hours of sleep
  • Wind-down routine: dim lights, stretching, journalling
Stress Level
/100
—
What it means

Physiological stress level is derived from HRV patterns across the day — when the autonomic nervous system shifts toward sympathetic (fight-or-flight) dominance, stress rises. Chronic high stress elevates cortisol, which breaks down muscle tissue, suppresses immune function, and impairs sleep quality. Stress and training load compete for the same recovery resources.

Reading the scale
  • 0–25: Resting state — ideal for adaptation
  • 26–50: Moderate — body handling demands well
  • 51–75: High — recovery and sleep will suffer
  • 76–100: Very high — reduce load immediately
How to reduce
  • Box breathing: 4 s in, 4 s hold, 4 s out, 4 s hold
  • 10–20 min walk outdoors — nature exposure lowers cortisol
  • Cold water face immersion activates the dive reflex and lowers HR rapidly
  • Reduce training load — accumulated stress and workout stress stack
  • Prioritise sleep — even one additional hour meaningfully reduces daily stress load
Vital Signs
Blood Pressure
/ mmHg
—
What it means

Blood pressure is the force blood exerts on artery walls. Systolic (top number) is the peak pressure during a heartbeat; diastolic (bottom) is resting pressure between beats. Sustained high blood pressure accelerates arterial damage, increasing risk of heart attack and stroke. Aerobic exercise is one of the most evidence-based interventions — 3–5 sessions per week can reduce systolic BP by 5–8 mmHg.

AHA categories
  • Normal: <120 / <80 mmHg
  • Elevated: 120–129 / <80 mmHg
  • Stage 1: 130–139 or 80–89 mmHg
  • Stage 2: ≥140 or ≥90 mmHg
  • Crisis: >180 or >120 mmHg
How to improve
  • 150+ min of moderate aerobic exercise per week
  • Reduce sodium intake — target <2,300 mg/day
  • DASH diet: high in vegetables, fruits, whole grains, lean protein
  • Limit alcohol to <2 drinks/day
  • Reduce chronic stress — cortisol raises blood pressure
  • Maintain healthy body weight
SpO₂ — Blood Oxygen Saturation
%
—
What it means

SpO₂ measures the percentage of haemoglobin in your blood that is carrying oxygen. At altitude, during illness, or with sleep-disordered breathing, SpO₂ drops — directly impairing power output and cognitive function. During altitude camps, athletes monitor SpO₂ to ensure adaptation is occurring without compromising performance. A reading below 95% at sea level warrants attention.

How to optimise
  • Measure at true rest — activity temporarily lowers readings
  • Nasal breathing at rest and during low-intensity exercise improves O₂ uptake
  • Diaphragmatic breathing exercises strengthen respiratory efficiency
  • Address sleep apnoea — nocturnal desaturations impair recovery significantly
  • Altitude acclimatisation protocols for high-altitude training camps
Resting Respiratory Rate
breaths/min
—
What it means

Resting respiratory rate (measured over 60 seconds at complete rest) is a sensitive early biomarker for recovery status and acute illness. Elite aerobic athletes commonly exhibit resting rates of 10–14 br/min due to improved breathing mechanics and higher lung volumes. A sudden increase of 2–3 br/min above baseline can signal the onset of illness or significant overtraining before other symptoms appear.

How to optimise
  • Practice slow breathing — 5–6 breaths/min during rest (coherence breathing)
  • Nasal breathing activates the diaphragm and improves efficiency
  • Yoga and Pilates improve respiratory muscle strength and control
  • Sustained cardio lowers resting rate over weeks and months
  • Address anxiety — chronic hyperventilation elevates resting rate

What it means

Body fat percentage separates functional lean mass (muscle, bone, organ) from fat mass. Unlike scale weight, it determines your power-to-weight ratio — the key performance variable in running, cycling, and any sport requiring you to move your bodyweight. The Navy circumference formula requires only a tape measure and is accurate to ±3–4% compared to DEXA scanning.

Categories by sex
  • Male: Essential <6% · Athletic 6–13% · Fitness 14–17% · Acceptable 18–24% · Obese ≥25%
  • Female: Essential <13% · Athletic 14–20% · Fitness 21–24% · Acceptable 25–31% · Obese ≥32%
  • Athletes in power-to-weight sports target the low end of their athletic range
How to improve
  • Caloric deficit of 300–500 kcal/day — larger deficits sacrifice muscle
  • High protein intake (1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight) preserves lean mass during a cut
  • Resistance training 3–4× per week protects and builds muscle tissue
  • Zone 2 cardio accelerates fat oxidation without cortisol-driven muscle loss
  • Sleep 7–9 hours — growth hormone (primary fat-burning hormone) peaks in deep sleep
Waist-to-Height Ratio
— ratio
Calculated from your waist (entered in Body Fat above) and height.
—
What it means

Waist-to-height ratio predicts cardiometabolic risk more reliably than BMI because it captures central (abdominal) adiposity. Fat stored around the abdomen is metabolically active — it releases inflammatory cytokines and drives insulin resistance. The threshold of 0.50 (your waist = half your height) is validated across populations and sexes as the point where risk begins to meaningfully increase.

How to improve
  • Target WHtR below 0.50 — "keep your waist less than half your height"
  • Visceral fat responds faster to exercise than subcutaneous fat
  • High-intensity interval training (HIIT) preferentially targets visceral fat
  • Reduce refined carbohydrates and sugars — primary drivers of visceral accumulation
  • Sleep deprivation increases visceral fat independently of diet and exercise
